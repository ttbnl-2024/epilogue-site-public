Solution: Melody Medley
Answer: GLASS FLUTE

Written by Wesley "Adalbert Zollern" Zhang

The puzzle is presented as a mashup work of (Western) classical music. It is actually a five-puzzle minipuzzle set, with boundaries defined by the boxed letters.

The symbols on the score are colored one of four ways:

  • Sections relevant to the puzzle are colored black, and are generally the tune to some major classical work (though the mashup tweaks key, tempo, and time signature to match the atmosphere of the mashup at the time, the contour and general rhythm should still be recognizable).
  • Accompaniment and other parts irrelevant to the puzzle are notated in grey. (They're really just there to make the whole thing sound nice, though they will generally be inspired by the accompaniment of a nearby work.)
  • Parts of the medley that contain hints are colored in blue.
  • There are twelve red symbols, whose role will be explained later in this solution.

As an identification aid, and to help determine where one melody transitions to another, the initials for the composer of each melody are provided above the first note.1

Each minipuzzle, labeled as sections [A] through [E], takes place in an entirely musical setting, and will resolve to a single melody, also from classical music. Solutions to these are presented in the appendix.

It's not necessary to identify the source, but it does make it easier to find.

The material not marked A-E is marked M with a number, each of the five sections consisting of three melodic lines in some arrangement (and accompaniment); all three lines are marked with ??. One is always the same: the main theme to Edward Elgar's Enigma Variations, as clued by the subtitle and dedication. The other two are taken from one of the variations, each one marked with a number 1 to 14. (The most famous variation, Nimrod (IX), is the main melody in the second such section.)

The ending credits card also provides an acknowledgement for an unknown figure in red text, bookended by a treble clef and a half note. These happen to be the first and last red symbols in the sheet, suggesting writing the red symbols out in order.

Assembling them as such and reading them as English letters spells out GRANT WOOLARD.

Grant Woolard's YouTube channel hosts four mashups of classical music. Besides being a great source for identifying many (though not all) of the melodies used in this puzzle, it also features each answer melody in the puzzle exactly once3 (possibly transposed) in a spatial/temporal context matching that of one of the Enigma themes:

Author’s Notes

There's not a lot of classical (or other art music, to be complete) in the music puzzle scene. There's probably reasons for that—classical is by far the hardest genre to research, since Musipedia (suggested by the last hunt puzzle to use a classical work, however token) has very low accuracy and I can't seem to find any other search engines for these (not to mention the lack of lyrics). I tried my best to pick either pieces in the public consciousness or pieces in the mashups, or otherwise cant towards easy discovery methods (there are only so many pieces with place names); this also meant I tended to select for melodies near the beginning of a piece for easy verification. But hey, being unable to use a search engine certainly didn't stop the community from pooling together an answer key for Grant's mashups.

In the end, I think the range of puzzles here covers the field and the set of famous composers well enough1—here's to more.

You might notice that the answer to this puzzle is an answer in the Sedona round—Hygieia is one of Apollo's grandchildren, after all.

The initials for the composer hints are a nod to the Enigma Variations (which puts the initials of each referenced person as a subtitle).

Appendix: Minipuzzle Solutions

Section A - Unfinished Symphony

Backing track: F. Schubert - Symphony No. 8 "Unfinished", Mov. 1 - several themes

Schubert's themes (Pianos 2 and 3) are superimposed on a number of other well-known melodies (Piano 1). Each theme on Piano 1 abruptly cuts out to a rest before its last note is played, as if unfinished. This tends to be the tonic or dominant of the key the piece happens to be in, which doesn't form a cogent melody. Referencing that note in the work's original key, as suggested by the hint, reveals the missing notes that compose the answer, which is a different theme from the Unfinished Symphony.

Section B - Song of the Earth

Initial theme: G. Mahler - Das Lied von der Erde, I. "Das Trinklied vom Jammer der Erde"

After the Mahler and C. P. E. Bach's Solfeggio in C minor, this section features a number of pieces with a European location in the title; at any given point, one is on piano 1 and one is on piano 2, with all accompaniment played on piano 3.

First, divide these into groups based on continuity. If piano 1 is playing the same melody while piano 2 switches pieces, these are in the same group; the same applies the other way around. Only when both pianos switch melodies does a new group start. For simplicity, these groups are all four measures long.

Then, for each group, draw a line between any two pieces playing together on a map.

There are sixteen groups (including repeat symbols) of seven distinct types. These form the following symbols:

Several non-Mahler parts of this piece are colored blue: C. P. E. Bach's Solfeggio in C Minor, hinting towards solfège, and three notes at the end notated as belonging to "F. Sud.". The notes read G-D-G, or "sol-re-sol", in fixed-do solfège. These point at the constructed language Solresol (created by François Sudre), which has a symbol convention for each of the seven notes of the diatonic scale; here, however, they are used for their solfège note value, and not for forming Solresol words. This obtains "mi fa mi re do re do ti la ti la la so mi re do", a melodic line from La traviata.3

Section C - Rite of Spring

Initial theme: I. Stravinsky - Le Sacre du printemps, Danse sacrale

The time signatures are noticeably wacky here and out of time with the melodies. To reinforce this, the "hint" theme is the Sacrifical Dance from the Rite of Spring, well-known for frequently changing its time signature.

Reading the time signature numerators as A1Z26 reveals the phrase CROTCHETS PER BAR READ AS JIANPU. This cues a look at the actual time signature of each other piece used:

Work Time
M. Ravel - Boléro 3/4
G. F. Handel - Keyboard Suite No. 7 in G minor - 6. Passacaglia 4/4
A. Vivaldi - L'estate ("Summer"), III. Presto agitato 3/4
F. Schubert - String Quartet No. 14 "Death and the Maiden", IV. Presto 6/8
S. Prokofiev - Romeo and Juliet - Dance of the Knights4 4/4
G. Verdi - Requiem - Dies irae 4/4
G. Holst - The Planets - Mars, Bringer of War 5/4

Taking the actual time signatures of the pieces and counting the number of quarter notes ("crotchets") in each bar (equivalent to converting the fraction to a denominator of 4) yields 3 4 3 3 4 4 5. This is jianpu (numbered musical notation, also known as "simplified notation") for the main theme of the third movement of Vivaldi's La primavera ("Spring").

Section D - Well-Tempered Clavier

Backing track: J. S. Bach - Das wolhtemperierte Klavier; BWV 846-893

This section is all about intervals. (The choice of backing track is a play on temperament, or a tuning system based around intervals.)

In any given section, the piece notated in blue is the beginning of a prelude from The Well-Tempered Clavier, by J. S. Bach. The other piece is another famous work, either for solo piano or a piano concerto.

While the two pieces are once again transposed to the home key of the full work, the keys of the original works are in most cases different; take the interval between the two, viewed from the perspective of the Bach piece (modulo octaves). Depending on whether the Bach work is played above or below the other work, this will be an interval up or down. (The hint for this is near the beginning of the section: an arrow pointing between the key signatures5 and an accent symbol resembling a Greek letter delta).

Order these intervals by the book and prelude number. The intervals produce a later theme from Felix Mendelssohn's "Frühlingslied" (Spring Song).

Section E - Rhapsody in Blue

Backing track: G. Gershwin - Rhapsody in Blue

This short coda does not require actual classical music knowledge, as the only piece used is the "stride" theme from the coda of Rhapsody in Blue. The only differences are omissions in some of the chords.

The only blue notes here are in the chords on Piano 3; they spell out "OTHER TYPE" in Braille. The chords during the stride itself always play the upper and lower notes (forming an octave), while the middle notes vary, some present, some absent:

. .'.:  .':' '  .'':  .'''  .'.:  .':' '  .'':  .'''
b B-4 ||B-8  .||B-16||B-2 ||B-4 ||B-8  .||B-16||B-2

As hinted by the Braille chords and the usage of Rhapsody in Blue (often described as a musical portrait of New York), this is New York Point, a Braille-like dot alphabet with a method for encoding musical notation. Translating this segment out of New York Point gives eight B flat notes played in the rhythm quarter-dotted eighth-sixteenth-half. This is Chopin's Marche funebre, the famous third movement from his Piano Sonata No. 2.

(The other two parts in this section are Movement 3 of Rachmaninoff's Piano Concerto No. 2 and Movement 6 of Albeniz's Recuerdos de viaje, to match the ending to Classical Music Mashup IV.)
